MARSHA P. JOHNSON

(1945-1992)

Marsha “Pay it no Mind” Johnson, a transgender woman, was an LGBTQ+ history icon who helped spark the Stonewall Riots in 1969. The Stonewall Riots marked the beginning of a new and powerful phase in the movement for LGBT rights. With Sylvia Rivera, another transgender activist, Marsha established the Street Transvestite Action Revolutionaries (S.T.A.R.), an organization dedicated to helping the homeless LGBT youth of New York City; they provided a safe-space for them along with shelter and support. Marsha was tragically murdered in 1992, however, authorities deemed her death a suicide, and her case was quickly closed.

SYLVIA RIVERA

(1951-2002)

Sylvia Rivera was a self-identified drag queen, and one of the most prominent transgender activists in history. Alongside fellow LGBTQ+ activists, at the young age of seventeen, she helped incite the Stonewall Riots; this riot helped lead to monumental progressions in the fight for LGBTQ+ rights. She was very involved in helping her community, for example, Sylvia and her friend Marsha P. Johnson, another LGBT history icon, established the Street Transvestite Action Revolutionaries (S.T.A.R.). Sylvia was also the co-founder of the Gay Liberation Front and the Gay Activists Alliance in New York City. All of these organizations were dedicated to fighting for LGBT rights and supporting the LGBT youth.

ALAN TURING

(1912-1954)

Alan Turing is often named as the father of computer science. He studied mathematics at King’s College and Princeton. During World War II, he was recruited into British communications. He created a device called the Bombe Machine that could decrypt the German’s Enigma code. His achievements during the war were kept secret, so he didn’t receive credit for his influential work. In 1948, he began doing research for computer development at Manchester University. In 1952, Turning was arrested for having an affair with another man. He was forced to take oestrogen injections, intended to suppress his attraction to men, to avoid jail time. He died in 1954 from cyanide poisoning, which is often ruled as a suicide.

BAYARD RUSTIN

(1912-1987)

Bayard Rustin was a gay civil rights leader who organized the March on Washington. He contributed to the Civil Rights Movement, Peace Movement, and Gay Rights Movement. He worked with Martin Luther King Jr., and helped him organize protests and supported him with the Montgomery Bus Boycott. Rustin and King stopped working together when Rep. Adam Clayton Powell Jr. told King that if he didn’t stop working with Rustin, that Powell would tell the press that King and Rustin were gay lovers. King called off the march and distanced himself from Rustin, resulting in Rustin’s resignation from King’s Southern Christian Leadership Conference.

STORMÉ DELARVERIE

(1920-2014)

Stormé DeLarverie, a lesbian LGBTQ+ activist, was rumored to have thrown the first punch that incited the Stonewall Riots in New York. Two weeks after the Stonewall Riots, she helped form the Stonewall Veterans Association. DeLarverie was extremely active in her community and went to numerous pride parades. Throughout her life she performed drag and was one of the first instigators of cross dressing.

HARVEY MILK

(1930-1978)

Harvey Milk was one of the first openly gay elected official in the U.S. In 1977, he was elected to San Francisco’s Board of Supervisors. The year after he was elected, he, and the city’s mayor, were shot and killed by a former city supervisor named Dan White. At the murder trial, White’s lawyers argued that he had not been in a good headspace due to clinical depression and the consumption of junk food the night before the murder. White faced a lesser charge of voluntary manslaughter and not first degree murder.

EDITH WINDSOR

(1929-2017)

Edith Windsor was an LGBT+ rights activist and the lead plaintiff in the 2013 case, the United States v. Windsor. She was married to her long term partner, Thea Clara Spyer, in 2007 in another country, and when Spyer passed away in 2009, Windsor went to court to obtain a tax refund. Since same sex marriage wasn’t legally accepted in the US, the courts refused her since she and her late wife weren’t seen as married because of the Defense of Marriage Act of 1996. Windsor sued as only recognizing heterosexual marriages and not homosexual marriages was unconstitutional. The Supreme Court overturned the Defense of Marriage Act of 1996 in a 5-4 ruling as “no person shall be ‘deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law’” (The New York Times).

LAVERNE COX

(1963-PRESENT)

Laverne Cox is a trangender woman, actress, and a modern day LGBTQ+ rights activist. She is most well known for playing Sophia Burset in Netflix’s popular series, Orange is the New Black. Cox is the first openly-known transgender person to be nominate for an Emmy, thus paving the way for more oppertunities for LGBT people.

ALEXYA SALVADOR

(1963-PRESENT)

Alexya Salvador is a Brazilian transgender woman and activist. She is the first trans pastor in Brazil and the first trans woman to adopt a child in Brazil. In 2018, she ran for the office of state representative.

ELISA LORIGA AND MARCELA IBEAS

(1862-Unknown) and (1867-Unknown)

Elisa Loriga and Marcela Ibeas became the first same sex marriage in Spain in 1901 by tricking the Spanish Catholic Church. They met when training to be teachers and reunited by teaching at schools nearby each other after Marcela’s family was concerned about her getting into a relationship and she travelled back to them. They both devised a plan to get married, by having Marcela get pregnant and announce that she was to marry Elisa’s “cousin Mario”. Elisa disguised herself as a man named Mario, and was baptised and married to Marcela on the same day. Their wedding photo was put on the local newspaper and they were outed and had to run from Spain in order to not face trial and serve jail time. Their wedding still stands as legal today.

VIRGINIA WOOLF

(1882-1941)

Virginia Woolf was a monumental writer and author who pioneered in transforming literature throughout her life. She is best known for her works of literature such as Mrs. Dalloway and To the Lighthouse. While she may not be explicitly labeled as Queer, she was known to have queer romantic relationships. Several of her novels and characters were written based on people who she had close relationships with; many of whom being women she was romantically with. Throughout her works she blends much of her own life in, she often passionately writes about her complex emotions and mental health struggles.

CHEVALIER D’ÉON

(1728-1810)

Charles-Geneviève-Louis-Auguste-André-Timothée d’Éon de Beaumont, also known as Chevalier d'Éon, Beaumont was a French spy and diplomat in the King’s service. D'Éon left France as a man in 1762 to spy for the French, and came back in 1777 as a woman. D'Éon was admitted into the royal and military Order of Saint Louis, and was allowed to be referred to as Chevalier. D'Éon was exiled from France to London until King Louis XV died in 1774, and his son issued Pierre Beaumarchais to represent the French government. After long discussion, d'Éon signed an agreement that all of the spy work the Chevalier possessed would be returned and she would return to France, and in return the king would pay some of the Chevalier’s debt and publicly recognize d'Éon as a woman.
